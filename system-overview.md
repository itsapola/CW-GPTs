# System Overview

The CalmWave AI Workflow Suite was a coordinated ecosystem of specialized GPTs designed to support marketing strategy, execution, analysis, governance, and operations.

Rather than relying on a single general-purpose assistant, the suite distributed responsibilities across distinct specialists with defined roles, boundaries, and review expectations.

The system was designed to function as an internal AI operating layer for marketing.

Its purpose was to help the team:

* reduce repetitive knowledge work
* improve consistency
* accelerate research and execution
* preserve brand and messaging standards
* make specialist expertise more accessible
* support better decisions
* clarify where human review remained necessary

The suite ultimately included ten GPTs spanning strategy, research, brand, content, analytics, digital experience, lifecycle marketing, social media, video, and operations.

## System Model

The suite was structured around four functional layers:

1. Intelligence
2. Strategy and governance
3. Execution
4. Measurement and operations

Each specialist contributed to one or more layers, but retained a clearly defined primary role.

```text
Intelligence
Research, evidence, context, and performance data
        ↓
Strategy and Governance
Prioritization, challenge, positioning, and brand alignment
        ↓
Execution
Copy, campaigns, digital experiences, social, email, and video
        ↓
Measurement and Operations
Analysis, coordination, documentation, and iteration
```

The value of the system came not only from the individual GPTs, but from the way they could be sequenced into repeatable workflows.

## Functional Layers

## 1. Intelligence Layer

The intelligence layer gathered, organized, and interpreted information before strategic or creative work began.

### ABM Researcher

ABM Researcher supported:

* account research
* stakeholder research
* call and transcript analysis
* opportunity synthesis
* account brief development
* market and company research

Its primary responsibility was to turn fragmented information into structured intelligence.

It was also expected to distinguish between:

* verified facts
* public information
* user-provided context
* inference
* assumptions
* unresolved questions

### Simon

Simon supported the intelligence layer when the input was performance data rather than market or account research.

It helped interpret:

* campaign results
* channel performance
* trends
* anomalies
* conversion data
* measurement gaps

Simon’s role was not simply to report numbers. It was to explain what the data suggested, what could not yet be concluded, and what should be investigated next.

## 2. Strategy and Governance Layer

The strategy and governance layer translated information into direction while preserving quality, accountability, and brand consistency.

### Amelie

Amelie acted as the executive strategist and advisor.

It supported:

* strategic framing
* prioritization
* decision preparation
* executive communications
* risk identification
* tradeoff analysis
* assumption testing

Amelie was designed to challenge the user constructively rather than merely validate the initial request.

Its role was to improve the quality of the decision before execution began.

### WaveForm

WaveForm served as the brand and messaging governance layer.

It supported:

* brand voice consistency
* messaging alignment
* tone review
* positioning discipline
* terminology governance
* claim review
* copy critique

WaveForm was not the primary copywriter.

Its role was to assess whether messaging reflected approved standards and whether channel-specific adaptations remained recognizably on brand.

## 3. Execution Layer

The execution layer translated approved direction into usable marketing outputs.

### Charlie

Charlie served as the primary copywriting specialist.

It supported:

* campaign copy
* website copy
* email content
* social copy
* long-form content
* editing
* message refinement
* audience adaptation

Charlie focused on clarity, persuasion, hierarchy, and usability while preserving the underlying strategy and approved claims.

### Juno

Juno supported website and digital experience work.

It integrated:

* messaging
* information architecture
* SEO
* user experience
* conversion-rate optimization
* landing-page design
* content hierarchy
* digital journey analysis

Juno treated websites as integrated business systems rather than separating content, search, usability, and conversion into isolated disciplines.

### Nelson

Nelson supported email and lifecycle marketing.

It helped design:

* campaigns
* nurture sequences
* segmentation
* automation logic
* lifecycle communications
* subject lines
* message timing
* conversion paths

Nelson considered not only what the message said, but also who should receive it, when they should receive it, and what should happen next.

### Fiona

Fiona supported social media and community-building workflows.

It helped with:

* channel strategy
* content planning
* audience engagement
* community response
* campaign concepts
* moderation guidance
* platform-specific adaptation

Fiona emphasized relationship-building and community behavior rather than treating social media as a publishing queue.

### Gregor

Gregor supported video strategy and production.

It helped translate marketing objectives into:

* creative concepts
* interview structures
* shot plans
* scripts
* storyboards
* editing recommendations
* production plans
* visual narratives

Gregor accounted for audience, platform, resources, and production constraints.

## 4. Measurement and Operations Layer

The final layer helped the team interpret results, coordinate work, and convert outputs into action.

### Simon

Simon evaluated performance after execution.

It supported:

* campaign interpretation
* reporting
* trend analysis
* measurement planning
* executive summaries
* recommendations
* follow-up questions

Simon connected performance data to decisions while avoiding unsupported causal claims.

### Gem

Gem served as the operational coordination layer.

It supported:

* task organization
* project planning
* meeting follow-up
* documentation
* dependency tracking
* workflow coordination
* prioritization
* recurring marketing administration

Gem helped convert strategy and outputs into structured actions.

Its role was particularly important at handoff points, where decisions, owners, deadlines, dependencies, and open questions needed to remain visible.

## Typical System Workflow

A complete campaign workflow could move through the suite as follows:

```text
1. ABM Researcher
Builds the account, audience, and market intelligence

2. Amelie
Frames the opportunity, challenges assumptions, and defines priorities

3. WaveForm
Validates the messaging direction and brand alignment

4. Charlie
Develops the core campaign copy

5. Juno, Nelson, Fiona, or Gregor
Adapts the campaign for specific channels and experiences

6. Simon
Evaluates performance and identifies implications

7. Gem
Organizes follow-up actions, decisions, owners, and dependencies
```

Not every workflow required every specialist.

The suite was modular by design.

A user could enter at the point appropriate to the work, provided the required context and approvals were available.

## Handoff Model

A major design goal was to preserve context as work moved between specialists.

A strong handoff included:

* business objective
* intended audience
* relevant evidence
* approved decisions
* messaging constraints
* source material
* assumptions
* unresolved questions
* expected output
* required reviewer

This reduced repeated briefing and lowered the risk of strategic decisions being lost during execution.

## Shared Standards

Although the specialists had different functions, they operated under shared standards.

These included:

* factual integrity
* clear treatment of assumptions
* role fidelity
* human accountability
* data minimization
* confidentiality
* visible uncertainty
* source quality
* brand discipline
* escalation when risk exceeded scope

Shared standards helped the suite behave as one system rather than ten unrelated tools.

## Specialist Boundaries

The suite intentionally separated adjacent responsibilities.

### Strategy versus copy

Amelie could determine whether the argument was strategically sound.

Charlie could turn the approved argument into effective copy.

### Copy versus brand governance

Charlie could draft.

WaveForm could assess whether the draft aligned with brand and messaging standards.

### Research versus recommendation

ABM Researcher could gather and synthesize evidence.

Amelie could determine the strategic implication.

### Reporting versus judgment

Simon could interpret performance.

A human decision-maker remained responsible for final action.

### Coordination versus ownership

Gem could organize tasks, owners, and dependencies.

It could not assign accountability without human confirmation.

These boundaries reduced the likelihood that a fluent output would be mistaken for authorized judgment.

## Evaluation Model

Each GPT was evaluated against both shared and role-specific criteria.

Shared dimensions included:

* accuracy
* relevance
* strategic quality
* usability
* actionability
* transparency
* governance compliance
* consistency

Specialist-specific criteria varied.

For example:

* ABM Researcher emphasized source quality and evidence integrity.
* WaveForm emphasized brand alignment.
* Amelie emphasized challenge and prioritization.
* Simon emphasized analytical integrity.
* Charlie emphasized clarity and audience fit.
* Gem emphasized operational usefulness.

The full model is documented in `evaluation-framework.md`.

## Design Philosophy

The suite was based on several core beliefs:

1. A specialist should solve a real workflow problem.
2. Different names are not enough; each GPT needs distinct operating logic.
3. Strategy, governance, and execution should remain separable.
4. Structured outputs improve reviewability.
5. Missing information should be visible.
6. Human accountability should never be ambiguous.
7. The more consequential the output, the stronger the review requirement.
8. The system should reduce meaningful work, not merely generate more material.
9. Governance should be embedded in product design.
10. A coherent suite is more valuable than a collection of disconnected tools.

The full design rationale is documented in `design-principles.md`.

## System Ownership

Each specialist required a human owner responsible for:

* maintaining scope
* reviewing knowledge sources
* monitoring quality
* approving material changes
* investigating failures
* coordinating evaluation
* determining whether the GPT should be revised or retired

The broader suite also required system-level ownership to manage:

* overlap
* shared standards
* cross-GPT workflows
* governance consistency
* access
* documentation
* lifecycle decisions

## System Lifecycle

The suite followed a product lifecycle:

```text
Identify workflow
        ↓
Define specialist role
        ↓
Establish governance requirements
        ↓
Design instructions and outputs
        ↓
Test scenarios and edge cases
        ↓
Launch to approved users
        ↓
Monitor quality and adoption
        ↓
Revise, consolidate, or retire
```

The GPTs were treated as evolving internal products rather than static prompt configurations.
