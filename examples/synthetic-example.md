# Synthetic Example

This example demonstrates how the CalmWave AI Workflow Suite could support a cross-functional marketing initiative from research through execution, measurement, and follow-up.

All company names, people, data, claims, and outputs in this example are fictional.

No CalmWave customer information, internal documents, proprietary prompts, protected health information, or confidential company data are included.

## Scenario

A fictional healthcare technology company, **Northstar Imaging**, is evaluating tools that could improve clinician communication and support faster interpretation of point-of-care imaging.

The marketing team wants to develop a targeted account-based campaign for Northstar’s clinical leadership team.

The campaign objective is to secure an introductory meeting with:

* the Chief Medical Officer
* the Vice President of Clinical Operations
* the Director of Innovation

The proposed campaign will include:

* an account brief
* strategic messaging
* a landing page
* a three-email sequence
* supporting LinkedIn content
* a short executive video
* a measurement plan
* an operational follow-up plan

## Synthetic Input

### Business objective

Secure a qualified introductory meeting with Northstar Imaging’s clinical and operational leadership team.

### Target audience

Primary:

* Chief Medical Officer
* Vice President of Clinical Operations

Secondary:

* Director of Innovation
* Clinical education leaders
* Procurement stakeholders

### Available information

* Northstar Imaging operates 18 outpatient imaging centers.
* The company has publicly discussed clinician efficiency and patient throughput.
* A recent executive interview referenced uneven adoption of new clinical tools.
* A fictional sales call suggested that training consistency may be a concern.
* No verified information is available about current budget, procurement timing, or active vendor evaluations.

### Constraints

* No clinical efficacy claims may be made without approved evidence.
* No patient outcomes may be implied.
* The campaign must avoid suggesting knowledge of confidential internal initiatives.
* The initial outreach should be educational rather than aggressively sales-led.
* All public-facing materials require human review.

## Stage 1: Account Intelligence

### Specialist

**ABM Researcher**

### Task

Develop an account brief using the available public information and fictional call notes.

Separate verified information, inference, assumptions, and unknowns.

### Example input

> Build an ABM brief for Northstar Imaging. Focus on clinician efficiency, tool adoption, and operational consistency. Do not infer budget, procurement activity, or clinical need without evidence.

### Synthetic output excerpt

## Northstar Imaging: Account Brief

### Verified information

* Northstar Imaging operates 18 outpatient imaging centers.
* Public leadership commentary has emphasized clinician efficiency and patient throughput.
* The organization has discussed the challenge of implementing new tools consistently across locations.

### User-provided information

* A fictional sales call indicated that training consistency may be a concern.
* The concern has not been independently verified.

### Working hypotheses

1. Northstar may value solutions that reduce variation across locations.
2. Clinical leadership may be more responsive to workflow consistency than to technology novelty.
3. Adoption and training may be more material than feature breadth.

### Unknowns

* Current clinical technology stack
* Active procurement initiatives
* Budget ownership
* Decision timeline
* Internal implementation capacity
* Current vendor relationships
* Formal success criteria

### Recommended research before outreach

* Confirm current leadership roles.
* Review recent public presentations and hiring activity.
* Identify published operational priorities.
* Validate whether clinician training is a current strategic issue.
* Avoid language implying knowledge of active internal projects.

### Governance note

The account brief contains hypotheses based on limited information. These should guide discovery questions, not be presented as confirmed facts.

## Stage 2: Strategic Framing

### Specialist

**Amelie**

### Task

Turn the account intelligence into a focused campaign strategy.

Challenge unsupported assumptions and define the strongest strategic angle.

### Example input

> Using the account brief, recommend the strongest campaign thesis. Identify the assumptions that could materially change the strategy.

### Synthetic output excerpt

## Strategic Recommendation

### Core opportunity

Lead with **consistent clinical adoption across distributed locations**, not with technology sophistication.

The available evidence suggests that Northstar’s potential challenge may be less about access to tools and more about consistent use, training, and operational integration.

### Campaign thesis

> Better tools create value only when clinicians can adopt them consistently across teams and locations.

### Why this angle is stronger

* It connects to Northstar’s public focus on efficiency.
* It addresses a plausible operational concern without asserting a confirmed internal problem.
* It creates room for a consultative conversation.
* It avoids unsupported claims about clinical outcomes.

### Material assumptions

* Training consistency is a meaningful concern.
* Clinical operations has influence over adoption decisions.
* Northstar is open to new workflow tools.
* The organization is not already committed to another implementation approach.

### Risks

* The campaign could feel presumptive if it overstates Northstar’s challenges.
* Messaging could drift into clinical claims without sufficient evidence.
* A technology-first message may undermine the operational thesis.
* The audience may interpret the campaign as generic if it lacks account-specific evidence.

### Recommended next action

Build the campaign around a diagnostic question:

> How do distributed clinical teams maintain consistent adoption when new tools, workflows, and training requirements vary by location?

## Human decision

A human marketing or account owner must confirm whether this thesis is appropriate before execution begins.

## Stage 3: Brand and Messaging Governance

### Specialist

**WaveForm**

### Task

Review the strategic direction for brand alignment, tone, unsupported claims, and messaging risk.

### Example input

> Review the proposed campaign thesis. Flag language that sounds inflated, presumptive, clinical, or unsupported.

### Synthetic output excerpt

## Messaging Review

### Approved direction

The operational-consistency angle is credible and appropriately consultative.

### Strengths

* Focuses on a real business challenge.
* Avoids exaggerated technology claims.
* Creates a relevant executive conversation.
* Supports both clinical and operational audiences.

### Language to avoid

Avoid:

* “Transform clinical outcomes”
* “Eliminate diagnostic variability”
* “Improve patient results”
* “Solve clinician adoption”
* “Northstar’s training challenge”

These phrases either imply unsupported outcomes or present an unverified hypothesis as fact.

### Preferred language

Use:

* “support more consistent adoption”
* “reduce friction in clinical workflows”
* “help teams implement new tools more consistently”
* “explore how distributed teams approach adoption”
* “support education and workflow integration”

### Tone guidance

The message should feel:

* informed
* restrained
* credible
* collaborative
* operationally aware

It should not feel:

* overly familiar
* diagnostic
* alarmist
* clinically authoritative
* aggressively sales-driven

### Governance decision

Proceed with the campaign direction, provided all copy preserves the distinction between a general industry challenge and a confirmed Northstar-specific issue.

**Charlie**

### Task

### Synthetic output excerpt

## Core Campaign Narrative

Clinical technology creates value only when teams can adopt it consistently.

For distributed healthcare organizations, that often means more than selecting the right tool. It requires clear education, practical workflows, and an implementation approach that works across locations, roles, and levels of experience.

This campaign invites Northstar Imaging’s clinical and operational leaders to explore how organizations can reduce friction between new technology and everyday clinical use—without adding unnecessary complexity.

### Primary headline

**Consistency is the real implementation challenge.**

### Supporting copy

New tools are only as effective as the workflows, education, and confidence surrounding them.

For organizations operating across multiple locations, adoption can vary even when the technology remains the same.

A more effective approach starts by designing for the realities of clinical use—not just technical deployment.

### Call to action

**Explore a practical approach to clinical adoption**

## Governance in Practice

This example demonstrates several governance principles.

### Facts remain separate from hypotheses

The account brief does not present training inconsistency as a confirmed Northstar problem.

### Strategy does not outrun the evidence

The campaign uses an industry-level operational challenge rather than asserting confidential or unverified knowledge.

### Creation and governance remain distinct

Charlie produces copy.

WaveForm reviews alignment and claims.

The human owner approves the final direction.

### Channel adaptation does not redefine strategy

Juno, Nelson, Fiona, and Gregor adapt the approved campaign thesis to their respective channels.

### Analytics avoids false certainty

Simon distinguishes engagement signals from business outcomes and causal conclusions.

### Operations preserves accountability

Gem organizes the work but does not assign final ownership without human confirmation.
